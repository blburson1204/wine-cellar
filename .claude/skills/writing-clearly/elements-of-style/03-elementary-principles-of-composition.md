# III. Elementary Principles of Composition

Paragraph structure, active voice, concision. These eleven rules govern how to
build clear, forceful prose.

---

## Rule 8. Make the paragraph the unit of composition: one paragraph to each topic.

If the subject is slight, a single paragraph suffices. Otherwise, subdivide into
topics, each in its own paragraph. The beginning of each paragraph signals to
the reader that a new step has been reached.

Single sentences should not be written as paragraphs, except sentences of
transition.

---

## Rule 9. Begin each paragraph with a topic sentence; end it in conformity with the beginning.

The most useful kind of paragraph:

(a) the topic sentence comes at or near the beginning; (b) the succeeding
sentences explain, establish, or develop the topic sentence; and (c) the final
sentence emphasizes the thought of the topic sentence or states an important
consequence.

Ending with a digression or unimportant detail is to be avoided.

---

## Rule 10. Use the active voice.

The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive:

| Passive                                                   | Active                                            |
| --------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me. | I shall always remember my first visit to Boston. |
| A survey of this region was made in 1900.                 | This region was surveyed in 1900.                 |

The habitual use of the active voice makes for forcible writing:

| Original                                                               | Revision                                         |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground.          | Dead leaves covered the ground.                  |
| The sound of a guitar somewhere in the house could be heard.           | Somewhere in the house a guitar hummed sleepily. |
| It was not long before he was very sorry that he had said what he had. | He soon repented his words.                      |

---

## Rule 11. Put statements in positive form.

Make definite assertions. Avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, non-committal
language. Use the word _not_ as a means of denial or in antithesis, never as a
means of evasion.

| Original                       | Revision              |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------- |
| He was not very often on time. | He usually came late. |
| not honest                     | dishonest             |
| not important                  | trifling              |
| did not remember               | forgot                |
| did not pay any attention to   | ignored               |

The antithesis of negative and positive is strong:

- Not charity, but simple justice.

---

## Rule 12. Use definite, specific, concrete language.

Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to
the abstract.

| Original                                                                | Revision                            |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| A period of unfavorable weather set in.                                 | It rained every day for a week.     |
| He showed satisfaction as he took possession of his well-earned reward. | He grinned as he pocketed the coin. |

The surest method of arousing the reader's attention is by being specific,
definite, and concrete.

---

## Rule 13. Omit needless words.

Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a
paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should
have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts.

| Original                           | Revision          |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------------- |
| the question as to whether         | whether           |
| there is no doubt but that         | no doubt          |
| used for fuel purposes             | used for fuel     |
| he is a man who                    | he                |
| in a hasty manner                  | hastily           |
| owing to the fact that             | since (because)   |
| in spite of the fact that          | though (although) |
| the fact that he had not succeeded | his failure       |

---

## Rule 14. Avoid a succession of loose sentences.

A series of sentences each consisting of two co-ordinate clauses joined by a
conjunction becomes monotonous. Recast enough to remove the monotony, replacing
them by simple sentences, sentences joined by semicolons, or periodic sentences.

---

## Rule 15. Express co-ordinate ideas in similar form.

Parallel construction requires that expressions of similar content and function
be outwardly similar:

| Original                                                                                          | Revision                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method, while now the laboratory method is employed. | Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method; now it is taught by the laboratory method. |

Correlative expressions (_both, and_; _not, but_; _not only, but also_; _either,
or_) should be followed by the same grammatical construction.

---

## Rule 16. Keep related words together.

The position of words in a sentence shows their relationship. Bring together
words related in thought; keep apart those that are not.

| Original                          | Revision                          |
| --------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| He only found two mistakes.       | He found only two mistakes.       |
| All the members were not present. | Not all the members were present. |

---

## Rule 17. In summaries, keep to one tense.

In summarizing action, use the present tense consistently. Shifting from one
tense to another gives the appearance of uncertainty.

---

## Rule 18. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.

The proper place for the word the writer desires to make most prominent is
usually the end:

| Original                                                                                              | Revision                                                                                             |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude since that time, though it has advanced in many other ways. | Humanity, since that time, has advanced in many other ways, but it has hardly advanced in fortitude. |

The other prominent position is the beginning. This principle applies equally to
sentences, paragraphs, and compositions.
